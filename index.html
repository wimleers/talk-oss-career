Drupal usage stats: '08‚Äì'11

Drupal 6 had an optional "update statistics" module. You had to manually install it. Drupal 7 included it. So: finally fairly accurate usage statistics!
Note how Drupal 6 usage went from 25K in June 2008 to 100K in December, to 200K in October 2009, to 300K mid 2010. Then it started to plateau, because Drupal 7 was around the corner. Then in June 2011, when important modules were ported, Drupal 8 started to take off even faster. From 25K to 300K in a year.

All of this to show that even the smallest projects can grow extremely big. It was originally something that a student (Dries, above, in Antwerp, Belgium!) started for dorm mates to discuss which parties to go to and for how many people to get pizza.

Drupal grew from a tiny funky hobby project to powering 2 to 3 percent of the web. Now it's powering whitehouse.gov, Harvard, NASA, Tesla and yes, the King of Belgium :)

Today's contributor is tomorrow's expert

Many people grew or even built their careers on Drupal! Because those hundreds of thousands of sites of course need to be built, maintained and supported. Plus, lessons are learned that must be applied to the next Drupal version.
People leave, people come. New contributors are stepping up all the time. Others leave, and go on to do great things elsewhere. Interests change, lives change, people change. But they left with more insights, more knowledge.

OSS = hiring strategy

Companies that are known to do this intentionally include GitHub, Facebook, Google and many more. In Belgium: not so much.
Specifically if your contributions stand out to for example Facebook employees working on the open source React or GraphQL, or Apple employees working on Swift!

Your GitHub profile overall (pull requests, forks), plus of course contributions to open source projects started/maintained by a company.
Unfair, because favors the privileged few: those who are rich enough to do unpaid labor, and have the time to do so. But it's a reality.

Better job thanks to OSS

Contributing to OSS, whether it's through code or peer reviews, constructive discussions or writing tests to make problems easier to reproduce‚Ä¶ it all adds up to very valuable experience. The sort of experience you will need in your job anyway.
So, contributing to OSS during your student years means you'll get a better job to start in (the more interesting job, or the better paying job, or both), and you'll be better at it sooner than those who did not contribute to OSS, because you've already got more experience!

In December 2006, I started using Drupal. 10 years later, I'm still contributing :) Bachelor thesis on making Drupal faster by using CDNs. Master thesis on using data mining for web performance optimization.
In the fall of 2011, I did an internship at Facebook in Silicon Valley, as the internship in the last year of CS. I got it thanks to my blogging about my bachelor & master theses.
In May 2012, I joined Acquia, a Drupal-based start-up, working remotely from Belgium.
This is me, at that very first DrupalCon. My first time abroad on my own. My first time flying. Sharing a room with a guy I had only ever talked to via IRC! But other people seemed to know him, so I took the risk. I had a superb time at DrupalCon!
I met Dries there, and that's how I got a Drupal summer job for the next 3 years: working for Mollom.com.
